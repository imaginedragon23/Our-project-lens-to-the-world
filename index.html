Film 1 : "Plane" (2023)

Synopsis:

"Plane" is an action-thriller directed by Jean-François Richet. The story follows commercial pilot Brodie Torrance (played by Gerard Butler) who makes an emergency landing on a remote island due to a severe storm. The passengers soon discover they have landed in a war zone controlled by dangerous militias. Torrance teams up with an accused murderer, Louis Gaspare (played by Mike Colter), to protect the passengers and ensure their survival.

Main Characters:

  • Brodie Torrance (played by Gerard Butler): A skilled and determined pilot whose leadership and quick thinking are crucial to navigating the crises.
  • Louis Gaspare (played by Mike Colter): A mysterious prisoner with a dark past who proves to be an unexpected ally in the fight for survival.
  • Scorpion Militias: The antagonists who capture the passengers, setting up the film's high-stakes rescue mission.

Detailed Analysis of Camera Shots:

The cinematography in Plane uses dynamic techniques to emphasize the film's intense action and emotional beats. - **Handheld Shots:** During turbulence and fight scenes, handheld cameras convey chaos and instability, immersing the audience in the action. - **Close-ups:** Frequent close-ups focus on the emotional stress of Brodie and the passengers, highlighting the stakes in their struggle for survival. - **Wide Shots:** Aerial and expansive wide shots of the island underscore the isolation and danger, contrasting with the confined space of the plane’s interior. - **Tracking Shots:** Used in action sequences, such as the firefights, to follow the characters and maintain suspense.

Sound Techniques:

The sound design enhances the tension and immersive experience: - **Ambient Sounds:** The roar of the storm, the creaking of the plane, and jungle sounds on the island immerse the audience in the environment. - **Dynamic Score:** Intense, rhythmic music heightens the action sequences, while quieter, suspenseful tones accompany moments of uncertainty. - **Silence:** Strategic use of silence, particularly after the crash, allows the audience to feel the gravity of the situation and provides contrast to the chaos. - **Dialogue Mixing:** The clear and balanced mixing ensures that critical exchanges between Torrance and Gaspare are audible even during high-stakes moments.

The combination of these elements creates a gripping and immersive narrative, keeping the audience engaged throughout the film.

Film 2 : "A story in Paris" (2000)

Synopsis :

In A story in Paris Mary is a brilliant student of architecture and lives in Paris. She has always looked up to her late grandfather, an architect of note. His soul searching to make four walls and a roof something special inspired her to follow the same course as her idol. A finalist in the most prestigious national design competition, she faces a difficult exam. Brilliant as she is and serious about it, Mary simply does not know what to do. Days blend into restless nights while her crystal-clear concept smudges with doubt. Frustrated and looking for clarity, Mary walks around the city that has always nurtured her. The streets that she knows so well feel different, weighted down with the burden of her creative block. Her steps lead her to an old workshop, her grandfather's sanctuary a place she hasn't visited since his passing years ago. Inside, the air is thick with dust and memories. She begins to explore, sifting through the blueprints and sketches that once captured his brilliance. As her fingers move over worn wooden surfaces, she chanced upon a small, rusted metal box hidden away in some obscure corner of the room. She opens it to reveal a letter with no other name than "Mary." Her breath caught. Dated 2000, the writing on this letter is so characteristic and so alive her grandfather's hand warm and familiar. As she reads, his words almost transcend time, with advice that feels eerily tailor-made for her present struggles. He speaks of creativity as something that comes along quite smoothly once one embraces imperfection and seeks inspiration from life itself. His reflections remind her to trust her instincts, to see beauty in simplicity, and to build not just with her mind but with her heart. Touched to tears, the spark in Mary reignites. That evening, she leaves the workshop with a new sense of determination. Over the next weeks, she works without rest on her design, imbuing every little detail with the wisdom of her grandfather and the sound of her voice. The result is a work that fuses modernity with timeless elegance; a design that breathes. And on this competition day, her work stands apart. The judges praise its originality and emotional resonance, calling it a masterpiece of form and meaning. They call out Mary's name for the winner, and she is overcome with pride and gratitude. Later, standing beside her finished model, Mary thinks about how everything changed with that letter. It was her grandfather's advice that helped her create something special but also reminded her why she chose this path. It was never just about winning; it was about connection, legacy, and building something that endures.

Main Characters:

  • Mary the architecture student: The protagonist and an amazing person that aims to defy the odds with her ambition and making the impossible possible
  • The omniscient: A shadowy person that invokes Mary's story and follow closely her difficult journey toward finding the right inspiration and winning the drawing competition.

Camera shot techniques:

  • 1. Crash Zoom (Mary s Lack of Inspiration): When the camera suddenly zooms in, it mirrors the overwhelming feeling Mary has as she struggles with her creative block. It's jarring and unexpected, just like the frustration building up inside her. The sharpness of the zoom brings attention to her emotions, making the audience feel her anxiety and mental chaos. It pulls them into her headspace, where everything seems magnified and out of control.
  • 2. Speed Burst (Accelerated Time Flow): The speed burst moves time forward, fast-tracking the moments leading up to the competition. It s as if the world around Mary is speeding up, forcing her to rush. This effect visually communicates how she feels the clock ticking, the pressure mounting. The quick transitions make it clear she s running out of time, and every second counts now more than ever.
  • 3. Omniscient View (Close Look and Emotional Fusion) The omniscient view lets the audience see everything from a broader perspective. Its not just about Mary s actions, but how she feels during those quiet moments. It allows the viewer to connect with her emotions in a more intimate way, almost as though they are inside her head. Watching her from above or from a distance helps fuse her emotional journey with the world around her, giving the film a deeper resonance.
  • 4. Centered Shot (Mary Walking Down the Streets) When Mary walks through the streets of Paris with the camera focused squarely on her, it highlights her isolation in the moment. The city around her seems to fade as the camera keeps her at the center of attention. Its a visual way of showing that, despite the busy world, she is on a path she must walk alone. This shot captures her sense of self-discovery and her struggle to find the way forward.
  • 5. Zoom Out and Upper Shot (Fade Out to Close the Film) As the camera pulls away, it creates a feeling of distance, almost like stepping back to get a clearer picture. The upper shot, combined with the zoom out, elevates the moment. Its like watching Mary from a higher place, symbolizing how far she is come. The view opens up, suggesting that she is ready to face whatever comes next, having gained a new perspective on both herself and her future.

Sound effect techniques:

  • 1. Distant City Sounds (Mary Walking Through Paris) The soft hum of Parisian streets murmured voices, distant footsteps, the occasional car wraps around Mary as she walks. These sounds feel distant yet close, reflecting her inner world. As she moves, they gently fade into the background, giving way to the silence that dominates her thoughts. The sounds of life go on, but she is disconnected, caught in her own quiet struggle.
  • 2. Rustling Paper (Discovering the Letter) A soft rustling sound as Mary opens the timeworn letter, the crinkle of the paper delicate yet purposeful. The quiet, almost sacred sound contrasts the chaos that is been building inside her, as though the world pauses just for a moment. Its a subtle cue, signaling the shift from her uncertainty to the spark of inspiration that iss about to ignite.
  • 3. Faint Clock Ticking (Passing Time) A faint clock ticking in the background serves as a constant reminder of the passing moments. Its barely audible at first, but as the deadline approaches, the ticking grows louder, more persistent. The sound keeps time with Mary s frantic pace, adding an auditory layer to her sense of urgency. Its the sound of time slipping through her fingers, and it gets under the audience's skin.
  • 4. Soft Wind (Inspiration and Discovery) As Mary uncovers her grandfather's letter, a light breeze swirls around her, symbolizing the fresh wave of inspiration rushing in. The wind is gentle but carries an almost magical quality, amplifying the moment of revelation. Its calming, yet full of potential, a sound that represents change and the start of something new for Mary.
  • 5. Building Sounds (Mary's Design Process) The sound of pencils scratching paper, the faint tap of her keyboard, the soft murmur of her thoughts. These everyday sounds ground her creative process, capturing her determination and focus. They are layered with soft, rhythmic intensity as her designs take shape, punctuated by the occasional, satisfying click of a model piece fitting into place. Its a sound of creation slow and steady but building toward something powerful.
